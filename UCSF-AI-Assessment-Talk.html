Section 1 · Framework

Four pillars of AI policy at UCLA

Each pillar reflects a distinct value the institution must protect as AI enters education and research. They organize both the survey design and the resulting course policy.

Pillar 1

Awareness & Literacy

Users understand what AI tools are, how they work, where they fail, and how to verify outputs. Without literacy, every other pillar collapses.

Pillar 2

Equitable Access

Capability cannot depend on ability to pay, prior coding background, or which lab a student joins. Policy must close gaps, not widen them.

Pillar 3

Innovation

AI is a real accelerator for teaching and discovery. Policy must enable experimentation rather than reflexively restrict.

Pillar 4

Research

Reproducibility, data handling, IP, human-subjects safeguards, and authorship must hold up under AI use.

Section 1.5 · Origin

BE elaboration by pillar

P1 Awareness & Literacy

Why this section: We move beyond general AI familiarity to discipline-specific judgment in bioengineering tasks. This section separates basic use, verification behavior, and perceived deskilling risk so instruction can target each one directly.

P1
Sections (bullet points)
  • Section A: Literacy by task. We ask whether students can interpret AI output quality and limits across data analysis, bioinformatics, imaging, and molecular modeling tasks.
  • Section I: Calibrated verification. This captures concrete checking behaviors (cross-checking sources, sanity checks, replication) rather than general trust attitudes.
  • Section K: Student-perceived deskilling risk. We assess whether frequent AI use is reducing confidence or independent performance on core BE analytic tasks.
Example questions (with section mapping)
  • A: I can evaluate whether an AI-generated bioinformatics result is biologically plausible.
  • I: I verify AI output with an independent source before using it in coursework.
  • K: Frequent AI use may weaken my ability to perform core analysis tasks independently.
P2 Equitable Access

Why this section: Equity gaps can hide behind overall adoption rates. We split access into computing, cost, preparation, and safe-use guidance so interventions can be matched to the actual barrier.

P2
Sections (bullet points)
  • Section F1: Computational resources. Access to devices, stable internet, and sufficient compute for AI-enabled coursework.
  • Section F2: Financial resources. Whether paid subscriptions, API credits, or premium features create unequal participation.
  • Section F3: Prior coding/ML experience as disadvantage. Whether students without prior technical background are structurally behind.
  • Section F4: Guidance on safe data use. Clarity on what data can be entered into external tools, and what is restricted.
Example questions (with section mapping)
  • F1: I have access to sufficient computing resources for AI-enabled coursework.
  • F2: Cost of paid AI tools creates a disadvantage for students in my program.
  • F4: I receive clear guidance on safe and appropriate data use with AI tools.
P3 Innovation

Why this section: Innovation is not just tool usage frequency. This section checks whether AI is improving conceptual learning and workflows, or merely substituting for core student reasoning.

P3
Sections (bullet points)
  • Section B: BE-specific tool use. Which domain tools students actually use and for which workflow stages.
  • Section D: Learning impact in BE contexts. Whether AI improves conceptual understanding, not just task speed.
  • Section E: BE-specific training needs. Where students need structured support, exemplars, or guided practice.
  • Section H: Augmentation vs. substitution. Whether AI supports student reasoning or replaces core cognitive work.
Example questions (with section mapping)
  • B: Which BE-specific AI tools have you used (e.g., AlphaFold, image analysis tools, coding assistants)?
  • D: AI tools have improved my understanding of core concepts in bioengineering courses.
  • H: I use AI primarily to augment my workflow rather than replace my own reasoning.
P4 Research

Why this section: Research integrity requires operational boundaries, not abstract warnings. These items translate data/IP constraints and reproducibility expectations into concrete, documentable practices.

P4
Sections (bullet points)
  • Section C4: Sensitive data and IP boundaries. Rules for unpublished data, human-subjects data, proprietary content, and external uploads.
  • Section J: Reproducibility and documentation. Required records of prompts, model/version, outputs, and verification steps.
  • Section B (research rows): Research-grade tool use. How students apply AI tools in lab workflows while maintaining methodological rigor.
Example questions (with section mapping)
  • C4: Is uploading unpublished or human-subjects data to external AI tools permitted, disclosed-only, or prohibited?
  • J: I document prompts, model/version, and verification steps when AI is used in research tasks.
  • B (research rows): Which research-grade AI tools are you using for lab analysis or modeling?

Pre-results planning

Other hypothesized signals and possible responses

Before results are released, we pre-specify additional "if-then" pathways so findings translate into guided use: better support, clearer workflows, and targeted guardrails.

Pillar 2 Access gap signal

If we observe
  • Students report disadvantages tied to cost or prior coding/ML background.
  • Resource inequities cluster by course type.
Guided responses
  • No paid tools required for graded tasks.
  • Baseline onboarding + prompt/workflow exemplars for underprepared students.
  • Rubrics score outcomes, not tool access.

Pillar 3 Over-reliance signal

If we observe
  • Students report gains in speed, but low confidence without AI support.
  • High dependence for core reasoning tasks.
Guided responses
  • Use a staged workflow: independent attempt, AI-assisted revision, then justification.
  • Require paired submission (baseline draft + AI-improved version) to show learning gain.
  • Use decision logs with feedback to coach augmentation over substitution.

Pillar 4 Research-risk signal

If we observe
  • Frequent uncertainty on data/IP boundaries.
  • Reproducibility concerns increase in AI-supported projects.
Guided responses
  • Publish an approved workflow map: what tools are allowed for each data type.
  • Require AI-use appendix (prompts, model/version, verification) with worked examples.
  • Embed reproducibility checklist in grading criteria and TA feedback.

Throughline hypothesis (pre-results)

If policy guidance is unclear, what do we do first?

One pre-results decision rule: if uncertainty is mainly about what is allowed, intervene with clearer task-level communication before adding stricter restrictions.

If we observe

Unclear policy signal

  • Students rate policy clarity as only moderate.
  • "Unsure" responses are frequent for high-risk activities (data, IP, human-subjects).
  • Confusion is about boundaries of use, not only attitudes toward AI.
→

Then we do

Communication-first interventions

  • Label assignments with Green / Yellow / Red usage rules.
  • Require disclosure statements on Yellow tasks.
  • Add explicit data-handling language to syllabus and rubrics.
Important: this is one possible signal pathway. Other survey signals (equity gaps, over-reliance, reproducibility concerns) would trigger different interventions.

Section 4 · Course-level redesign

Backward design

Begin with intended performance, not assignment format. Then align evidence and learning activities in that order.

1) Outcomes first

What should students be able to do by the end of the course?

→

2) Evidence next

What evidence would convince us they can actually do it?

→

3) Learning design

What learning experiences prepare students to produce that evidence?

Section 4 · Course-level redesign

Bloom taxonomy

Bloom helps faculty calibrate cognitive demand so assessment tasks target the level of thinking we care about.

Remember
Recall facts, definitions, and procedures. Useful for baseline knowledge checks.
Understand
Explain concepts in your own words and interpret meaning.
Apply
Use methods in a new problem context (e.g., apply a model to real data).
Analyze
Break down decisions, compare alternatives, and identify errors or assumptions.
Evaluate
Judge quality with criteria, defend choices, and critique evidence.
Create
Design or produce original work that integrates methods, evidence, and justification.
Why it matters in the AI era: AI can often generate passable outputs at lower levels (Remember/Understand). More reliable evidence of student learning usually comes from tasks that require Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, and Create in context.

Practical use: map each major assessment to a Bloom verb before finalizing the syllabus.

Section 4 · Course-level redesign

Traffic light policy

Use a clear AI-use posture per task so students know exactly what is allowed.

Green · Permitted

Brainstorming, studying, exploration, debugging your own code, generating practice problems. No disclosure required.

Yellow · Permitted with disclosure

Drafting prose, writing boilerplate code, generating figures, summarizing readings. AI-use statement is mandatory.

Red · Prohibited

Oral defenses, in-class quizzes, bench interviews, pre-registered analysis plans, reflective memos. Evidences your own reasoning.

Example 4 · Generic / Adaptable Template

Three layered evidence types

For any course, replace the originals with a triangulation that an AI alone cannot fabricate — because performance evidence requires the embodied student in the room.

Yellow

Process evidence

Drafts, notebooks, version-controlled commits, decision logs. Discloses AI use; values revision over polish.

13

Red

Performance evidence

Oral defense, bench interview, code-walk, design pitch with Q&A. The student's reasoning under questioning is the assessment.

1

Green

Reflection evidence

Structured reflections that ask the student to evaluate their own work, AI choices, and limitations. Often AI-permitted because the reflection itself is the cognitive task.

13

Bloom mapping: process touches Apply / Analyze · performance reaches Evaluate · reflection reaches Evaluate / Create.

Section 4 · Faculty checklist

Five questions before the syllabus goes live

Have I named at least one Red assessment per learning outcome?

Suggested answer: Define at least one no-AI performance task for each core outcome (e.g., oral defense, in-class explanation, or live problem-solving).

Pillar 1
Is every Yellow assessment paired with a disclosure mechanism (prompts, model, contribution)?

Suggested answer: Require a short AI-use statement listing tool/version, representative prompts, and what the student accepted/rejected.

Pillar 1Pillar 4
Does my Green category protect equity (no student is penalized for using or not using AI)?

Suggested answer: Keep Green tasks optional and grade the quality of the work product, not whether a student used AI tools.

Pillar 2
Does my data-handling clause cover unpublished data, human-subjects data, and partner IP?

Suggested answer: Explicitly state no external upload of sensitive or restricted materials, and include where approved alternatives are available.

Pillar 4
Have I aligned each assessment to a Bloom verb in the syllabus?

Suggested answer: Map each assessment to a target verb (Apply/Analyze/Evaluate/Create) so the task format matches the intended cognition.

Section 5 · Course AI policy

Drop-in syllabus language, organized by pillar

Pillar 1 · Awareness & Literacy

The traffic light + disclosure

Every graded task is labeled Green, Yellow, or Red. Yellow tasks require an "AI-use statement" — tool, version, prompts, what was kept vs. rejected. AI is not an author.

Pillar 2 · Equitable Access

No paid tools required

Free-tier or UC-licensed tools suffice for every Yellow task. Students who choose not to use AI on Yellow tasks are not penalized — the rubric grades the deliverable, not the toolchain.

Pillar 3 · Innovation

Use AI ambitiously on Green

Green list is illustrative, not exhaustive. Policy is a living document; updates with one week's notice and a written change summary, informed by mid-quarter feedback.

Pillar 4 · Research

Explicit data-handling clause

No external upload of unpublished data, human-subjects data, partner IP, or NDA/IRB material. Reproducibility documentation is a required deliverable on all research artifacts.

Why this layout: the audience can read the survey-to-policy logic directly off the syllabus. Each clause exists because a survey item said it had to.

Closing

For UCSF Epi/Biostats

The model is not "scrap your existing surveys and adopt ours." It is: use your campus-wide instrument as the trunk, grow a department-specific elaboration on the same architecture, organize both around the four pillars.

Start with one pillar

Pick the pillar most urgent for Epi/Biostats — likely Pillar 4 (Research integrity) given the data sensitivity of your work — and pilot the items there first.

Embed, don't add

Use the 10–14 item course-level mirror inside your existing end-of-course evaluation. Response burden stays low; signal aggregates upward to program-level on identical anchors.

Make policy an output, not an input

Let the first cycle's data write the next syllabus policy. Policy is a hypothesis tested against survey signal — not a permanent decree.
